What this shows — measurement as a bulk effect measurement ground · no φ

A 1D chain is driven by random entangling gates while, at each step, every qubit is projectively measured with probability p. The two processes compete: entangling dynamics build entanglement, projective measurements destroy it. The half-chain entanglement entropy S is the order parameter.

At small p the chain sits in a volume-law phase — S plateaus high, scaling with subsystem size. As p increases the curve collapses through a sharp transition region to an area-law phase, where S falls toward ~0: measurements win, and the steady state is essentially a product state. The steepest-drop marker locates the transition.

Where this sits: the measurement-induced phase transition is the measurement "ground" pole acting as a bulk effect — not a control trick on a single readout, but a genuine phase of the many-body dynamics. No φ here: this is the entropy/measurement counterpart, not the golden-order pole. Computed live in your browser via WebAssembly.

Honest scope. The chain is simulated. S is the trajectory-averaged half-chain von Neumann entanglement entropy (nats) of the post-measurement pure state, swept over the measurement rate p ∈ [0, 0.7]. With a finite chain the transition is rounded, not a sharp singularity; larger qubit counts sharpen it but cost exponentially more. Not realized hardware.
