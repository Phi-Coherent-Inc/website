What this shows — a real phase transition, located by finite-size scaling

A quantum circuit where entangling gates compete with projective measurements at rate p has a genuine phase transition: below a critical rate the steady state is volume-law entangled, above it the constant collapse forces an area-law (disentangled) state. To locate the transition you need finite-size scaling — and that needs large systems. Exact state-vector simulation caps out near 14 qubits, where the transition is washed out by finite size. So this runs in the Clifford setting, where Gottesman–Knill lets a stabilizer tableau reach 20 qubits exactly and a stabilizer state's entanglement entropy is a GF(2) matrix rank. No φ: measurement × entanglement physics.

The upper chart is the order parameter. At small p the half-chain entropy S(L/2) fans out — bigger systems hold more entanglement (volume-law). At large p the curves collapse together at a small value (area-law). The crossover is the transition.

The lower chart pins it down. The tripartite mutual information I₃ is dimensionless — scale-invariant exactly at the critical point — so the curves for different system sizes all cross at a single point. That crossing is the standard finite-size-scaling locator, and here it lands at pc ≈ 0.15, matching the known Clifford-MIPT critical value (~0.16) from the literature.
