What this shows — measuring a critical exponent by data collapse

Near a continuous phase transition, a dimensionless quantity obeys a finite-size scaling law: the tripartite mutual information of the measurement-induced transition satisfies I₃(p, L) = F((p − p_c)·L1/ν). So if you plot I₃ against the scaling variable x = (p − p_c)·L1/ν with the right critical point p_c and exponent ν, the separate curves for each system size L all fall onto one universal curve. No φ: measurement × entanglement physics.

Drag the sliders. Away from the right values the four colored curves (L = 12, 16, 20, 24) are spread apart; as you home in on p_c ≈ 0.15 and an O(1) exponent ν, they snap together. The "collapse quality" card measures how well they overlap (it compares each size's points against the others). The auto-fit button finds the (p_c, ν) that minimizes it for you.

The auto-fit recovers p_c ≈ 0.15 — the known Clifford-MIPT critical point — and an exponent ν of order one, consistent with the MIPT universality class (≈ 1.3); at these system sizes (L ≤ 24) ν is biased high by corrections to scaling, so we report it honestly as O(1) rather than a sharp 1.3. The crucial check: the collapse is real — its quality is many times worse at a wrong exponent, so the curves overlap only under genuine finite-size scaling.
